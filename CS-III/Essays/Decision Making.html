Introduction

Decision making biases are a common phenomenon in which individuals make irrational or illogical choices based on certain cognitive biases. These biases can affect the quality of decision making and lead to poor outcomes. Here are five common decision making biases:

Confirmation Bias

Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek out information that supports our existing beliefs and to ignore or discount information that contradicts those beliefs. This bias can lead to a lack of objectivity and an inability to consider alternative perspectives.

Anchoring Bias

Anchoring bias is the tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information we receive when making a decision. This can lead to a lack of flexibility and an inability to adapt to new information.

Groupthink

Groupthink is the phenomenon in which individuals within a group tend to conform to the dominant opinion of the group, even if that opinion is not supported by facts or evidence. This can lead to poor decision making and a lack of critical thinking within the group.

Loss Aversion

Loss aversion is the tendency to avoid taking risks and to prioritize avoiding losses over making gains. This can lead to a lack of innovation and a reluctance to pursue new opportunities.

Overconfidence Bias

Overconfidence bias is the tendency to overestimate our own abilities and to underestimate the risks associated with a decision. This can lead to poor decision making and a lack of humility.

Conclusion

Overall, these decision making biases can have negative consequences for individuals and organizations. It is important to be aware of these biases and to actively work to overcome them in order to make better, more rational decisions.
